Empathy in the Classroom

Is empathy a trait or a skill?

Some might consider empathy as a character trait—something a person innately possesses. Others may think it is more of a skill you can develop and improve. Which is it?

According to research, both are correct. Some people are intuitively better at sensing another person’s emotions than others. At the same time, people can always improve upon their ability to empathize with others; in that way, it is treated as a vital skill for improving interpersonal relations, as well as understanding people who are different from us.

How does empathy relate to my classroom?

Empathy is increasingly recognized as the key to successfully relating to others. Its prevalence in literature and research has grown steadily over time. Indeed, the number of journal articles as well as Google searches on empathy has increased dramatically in recent years.

In the context of education specifically, studies support the merit of empathy as a required skill for teachers to better understand the needs of their students, and to promote positive outcomes in the classroom.

As much as educators must know how to deliver curriculum to students, they must likewise possess the interpersonal skills needed to gain diverse perspective and better facilitate their students’ success. This is especially necessary with English Language Learners (ELLs).

Why? In large part, because many of your students will possess a cultural background different from your own. According to the Department of Education (2016), students of color are projected to comprise 56 percent of the student K- 12 population by the year 2024, yet the most recent data showed that 82 percent of public school teachers identified as white. Similar statistics on adult ESL instructors mirror these demographics.

Inevitably, many teachers come from different communities and experiences than their students; arguably, this places greater importance on preparing teachers for these differences and connecting with diverse students to ensure equitable learning opportunities (McAllister & Irvine, 2002).

ESL instructors with empathy prove effective in improving intercultural sensitivity toward their students. It is also important to note that you do not need to share the exact same experiences as another in order to achieve empathy. Empathy is a way to understand another person by learning their point of view.
