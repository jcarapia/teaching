What exactly is empathy?

The word “empathy” has lacked a precise definition since its origins. Empathy translates from Ancient Greek into two terms: empatheia (“physical affection”) and pathos (“passion” or “suffering”). The word first entered the English language as an incomplete translation of the German word Einfühlung (“to feel into”), which appeared in a doctoral thesis in 1873. The word was adapted to “empathy” by psychologist Edward Tichener in 1909. This history does little to clarify the term.

Take a look at the statements below. Based on your understanding of the concept, which of the statements defines empathy?

“Feeling what another person is feeling from their perspective.”

“Putting yourself in another person’s shoes.”

“Feeling another person’s pain.”

“Knowing the thoughts and feelings of another person.”

“Understanding and sharing the feelings of another person.”

“Awareness of the emotions of other people.”

“Understanding in a compassionate way.”

The answer is: all of these statements can describe empathy… depending on who you ask!

Empathy has been investigated in multiple fields, to include psychology, business, medicine, law, and education. It is described differently from field to field, and even within a particular area. Some researchers have gone so far as to divide the word empathy into eight distinct uses.

The definitions of empathy range from cognitive (knowing how another person thinks and feels) to emotional (feeling the way another feels), distressing (feeling distress upon observing another’s suffering), to compassionate (feeling for another’s suffering), and a number of areas in between.
